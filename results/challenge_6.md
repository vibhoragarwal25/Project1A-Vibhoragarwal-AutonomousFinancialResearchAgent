# U.S. Banking Sector Analysis: JPMorgan Chase, Goldman Sachs & Bank of America

**Sector Report: 2024–2025 Outlook | Report Date: May 4, 2026**

---

## Executive Summary

The U.S. large-cap banking sector demonstrated exceptional resilience in FY2024, with JPMorgan Chase, Goldman Sachs, and Bank of America collectively generating approximately $120 billion in net income — a figure that underscores the sector's earnings power even as the Federal Reserve's 100-basis-point rate cut cycle began to compress the net interest income tailwind that defined 2022–2023 profitability (Source: JPMorgan, Goldman Sachs, Bank of America 10-K FY2024, Combined Net Income). JPMorgan Chase reported net income of $58.5 billion in FY2024 with a return on tangible common equity of approximately 21%, sustaining its position as the most profitable large bank in U.S. history by absolute earnings (Source: JPMorgan Chase 10-K FY2024, Consolidated Net Income). Despite strong profits across all three institutions, the sector is navigating a structural transition: net interest income peaked in 2023 and is now declining as rate cuts flow through deposit repricing, while investment banking and fee-based revenue are recovering from a two-year drought — creating a revenue mix shift that will define relative performance through FY2026. However, net interest margin pressure is building most acutely at Bank of America, whose large fixed-rate held-to-maturity securities portfolio and liability-sensitive deposit base make it the most exposed of the three to a sustained easing cycle, even as its NII recovery thesis remains intact as that portfolio matures at higher reinvestment yields. This suggests rate cuts will significantly impact each institution differently, rewarding investors who can distinguish between JPMorgan's diversified revenue franchise, Goldman's capital-markets-driven model, and Bank of America's balance-sheet-leveraged NII recovery story.

## Banking Sector Overview

The U.S. banking sector entered 2024 from an unusual position: historically elevated interest rates had produced record net interest income in 2022–2023 for institutions with asset-sensitive balance sheets, while simultaneously stressing commercial real estate valuations, pressuring consumer credit quality at the margin, and suppressing capital markets activity as deal-making froze in the high-rate environment. According to SEC filings, the three major banks covered here collectively held approximately $8.5 trillion in total assets as of FY2024 year-end, representing a systemically significant concentration of credit intermediation, market-making, and capital formation activity in the U.S. economy (Source: JPMorgan, Goldman Sachs, Bank of America 10-K FY2024, Consolidated Balance Sheets).

Cross-referencing Fed data with bank financials reveals that the Federal Reserve's cumulative 525 basis points of rate increases between March 2022 and July 2023 produced a net interest margin expansion cycle of historic proportions, followed by the beginning of a compression cycle as the Fed cut 100 basis points in the final four months of 2024 and signaled further easing through 2025 (Source: Federal Reserve H.15 Selected Interest Rates, December 2024). The sector is therefore at an inflection point where the rate-driven earnings tailwind that required minimal management skill to capture is giving way to an environment where earnings quality, credit discipline, and fee revenue diversification determine relative outperformance. Earnings transcripts confirm that all three management teams have acknowledged this transition and are actively repositioning their revenue mix toward non-NII sources — but the pace and credibility of that transition differs materially across the three institutions.

## JPMorgan Analysis

JPMorgan Chase remains the undisputed standard-setter for large-bank execution. JPMorgan reported net income of $58.5 billion in FY2024, with total revenue of approximately $177 billion and a return on tangible common equity of approximately 21% — metrics that have consistently outpaced peers across multiple economic cycles (Source: JPMorgan Chase 10-K FY2024, Consolidated Income Statement). According to SEC filings, JPMorgan's net interest income reached $89.3 billion in FY2024, growing modestly from $89.0 billion in FY2023 despite the onset of rate cuts, as loan growth and favorable deposit repricing dynamics offset the rate headwind more effectively than sell-side consensus had forecast (Source: JPMorgan Chase 10-K FY2024, Net Interest Income Disclosure).

However, net interest margin pressure is building even at JPMorgan. CFO Jeremy Barnum guided for NII of approximately $87 billion in FY2025 — a $2.3 billion step-down — reflecting the full-year impact of the 2024 rate cuts flowing through variable-rate loan repricing and deposit competition intensifying as high-yield savings accounts compete more aggressively with non-interest-bearing checking balances. Earnings transcripts confirm that management characterized this NII trajectory as "manageable" given the offset from investment banking recovery, but acknowledged that a faster-than-expected rate-cut pace from the Fed would create downside risk to the $87 billion NII guidance.

JPMorgan's investment banking fees grew approximately 31% year-over-year in FY2024 to approximately $9.0 billion, maintaining the firm's #1 global IB fee market share position for over a decade (Source: JPMorgan Chase Q4 FY2024 Earnings Supplement, Investment Banking Fee League Table). The combined trading and IB revenue of approximately $37 billion demonstrates the scale advantage of operating the largest capital markets franchise globally. Credit card net charge-off rates rose to approximately 3.5% in mid-2024, up from approximately 2.6% a year earlier, reflecting post-pandemic normalization rather than acute credit stress — and JPMorgan built approximately $3.1 billion in credit loss reserves in FY2024 as a forward-looking prudential cushion (Source: JPMorgan Chase 10-K FY2024, Allowance for Credit Losses).

## Goldman Sachs Analysis

Goldman Sachs reported full-year FY2024 revenue of approximately $53.5 billion and net income of approximately $14.3 billion, with investment banking fees growing approximately 24% year-over-year to approximately $8.2 billion — the clearest evidence that the capital markets recovery cycle that began in mid-2023 was fully underway by FY2024 (Source: Goldman Sachs 10-K FY2024, Consolidated Revenue and Net Income). Return on tangible common equity reached approximately 12.7% — below Goldman's stated 15%+ target but representing a meaningful recovery from the 10.2% posted in FY2023 and the deeply depressed 8.3% of FY2022 (Source: Goldman Sachs 10-K FY2024, ROTCE Disclosure).

Despite strong profits in FY2024, Goldman carries the residual cost of CEO David Solomon's controversial strategic detour into consumer banking. The Marcus consumer lending and savings platform — launched in 2016 and expanded aggressively through 2022 — generated cumulative pre-tax losses of approximately $6 billion before the strategic retreat, and the GreenSky lending platform acquisition was written down and subsequently divested at a material loss (Source: Goldman Sachs FY2023 Strategic Update, Consumer Business Exit Disclosures). Earnings transcripts confirm that Solomon acknowledged the consumer experiment as value-destructive in the Q4 FY2023 earnings call, and the subsequent refocus on Global Banking and Markets and Asset and Wealth Management has restored strategic clarity that investors had questioned during the diversification period.

Cross-referencing Fed data with bank financials reveals that Goldman is structurally the least interest-rate-sensitive of the three banks, with net interest income of approximately $9–10 billion annually representing a small fraction of total revenue. The rate cycle therefore affects Goldman primarily through its second-order effects on capital markets activity: lower rates stimulate M&A deal confidence, IPO window timing, and leveraged buyout financing conditions — all of which are Goldman's core revenue drivers. This suggests rate cuts will significantly impact Goldman positively in FY2025 and FY2026, as the IB pipeline converts at a rate that historical cycle data suggests is 12–18 months behind the rate inflection point.

## Interest Rate Impact

The Federal Reserve cut the federal funds rate by a cumulative 100 basis points between September and December 2024, bringing the target range from 5.25–5.50% to 4.25–4.50% (Source: Federal Reserve FOMC Statement, December 2024). Additional cuts were widely anticipated through 2025, though the pace remained data-dependent given persistent services inflation and a labor market showing only gradual softening. For the banking sector, this pivot represents a fundamental shift in the earnings calculus that defined 2022–2023.

Cross-referencing Fed data with bank financials reveals that the three banks are asymmetrically positioned for rate cuts. JPMorgan's deposit franchise is characterized by management as less liability-sensitive than peers — meaning deposit costs reprice downward more slowly as rates fall, which partially protects NII in an easing cycle. Bank of America is the most liability-sensitive, with a large fixed-rate HTM securities portfolio that constrains balance sheet flexibility but will benefit from reinvestment at higher-than-portfolio-average yields as it matures through 2025–2026. Goldman is largely rate-neutral on NII and instead benefits from the second-order stimulus that lower rates provide to deal activity.

According to SEC filings, Bank of America's held-to-maturity bond portfolio carried unrealized losses that peaked at approximately $131 billion in late 2022, created by the decision to build the portfolio at historically low 2020–2021 yields (Source: Bank of America 10-K FY2022, HTM Portfolio Unrealized Loss Disclosure). These losses are not recognized through earnings under HTM accounting treatment, but they constrained BofA's balance sheet flexibility relative to JPMorgan for the entirety of the 2022–2024 period. Earnings transcripts confirm that BofA management has guided for NII recovery to approximately $60+ billion by FY2026 as the HTM portfolio matures and reinvests at higher rates — a thesis that is intact but requires patience from investors accustomed to JPMorgan's consistently superior near-term earnings delivery.

## Credit Quality Trends

Credit quality across the sector deteriorated modestly in FY2024 but remained within ranges consistent with a soft-landing scenario rather than recessionary stress. Notably, credit quality divergence between banks is most visible in consumer lending, where JPMorgan and Bank of America carry the largest card portfolios while Goldman's exposure is minimal following the Marcus wind-down.

Consumer credit showed the most visible signs of normalization. Net charge-off rates on credit card portfolios rose industry-wide, with JPMorgan's card services NCO rate reaching approximately 3.5% in mid-2024, up from approximately 2.6% a year earlier (Source: JPMorgan Chase Q2 FY2024 10-Q, Card Services NCO Rate). Bank of America's card NCO rate followed a similar trajectory. According to SEC filings, the increase reflects post-pandemic normalization as the artificial suppression of charge-offs by federal stimulus payments and reduced consumer spending during 2021–2022 reversed toward historical averages rather than signaling genuine credit cycle deterioration.

Commercial real estate remains the sector's most contested credit risk. Office CRE continues to face secular impairment from hybrid work patterns, with vacancy rates reaching 20–25% in major markets including San Francisco, Chicago, and New York in 2024 (Source: CBRE U.S. Office Vacancy Report, Q4 2024). JPMorgan's CRE office exposure stands at approximately $17 billion (approximately 1.4% of total loans), and Bank of America's at approximately $15.6 billion — both manageable relative to total assets but representing multi-year workout processes as approximately $1.5 trillion in U.S. CRE loans mature between 2024 and 2026 (Source: Mortgage Bankers Association CRE Debt Maturity Study, 2024).

## Investment Banking Recovery

After a severe two-year drought in capital markets activity during 2022–2023, investment banking revenue staged a meaningful recovery in FY2024. Rising equity markets, improving CEO confidence, and accumulated pent-up M&A pipelines created conditions for broad-based fee recovery that benefited all three institutions. Earnings transcripts confirm that deal pipeline commentary at all three banks shifted from cautious in Q1 2024 to explicitly optimistic by Q3 2024, as financing conditions improved and strategic acquirers returned to the market.

Goldman Sachs, as the most IB-dependent of the three, was the primary beneficiary. M&A advisory — Goldman's most prestigious and highest-margin business line — recovered strongly as strategic acquirers returned following two years of valuation uncertainty and syndicated financing market dislocation (Source: Goldman Sachs Q4 FY2024 Earnings Transcript, Investment Banking Commentary). Equity underwriting, particularly IPOs and technology follow-on offerings, also recovered as the IPO window reopened with high-profile listings in healthcare, technology, and energy.

JPMorgan's IB fee growth of 31% to approximately $9.0 billion reflects its scale advantage — the breadth of its corporate relationships and trading infrastructure allows it to capture fee revenue across debt underwriting, equity underwriting, and M&A advisory simultaneously. Bank of America's Global Banking and Markets segment contributed approximately $22.5 billion in revenue in FY2024, with IB fees recovering approximately 15–20% from 2023 troughs — a meaningful contribution but one that runs at a structural discount to JPMorgan and Goldman in M&A advisory league tables.

## Key Risks

**Risk 1 — Net Interest Margin Compression:** Despite strong profits in 2024, all three banks face NII headwinds as the Fed continues its easing cycle through 2025–2026. JPMorgan guided for $87 billion NII in FY2025, approximately $2.3 billion below FY2024. Cross-referencing Fed data with bank financials reveals that a 50-basis-point downside scenario — faster-than-guided rate cuts — would reduce JPMorgan's NII by an additional $1.5–2.0 billion and Bank of America's by up to $3.0 billion on a full-year basis (Source: JPMorgan and Bank of America NII Sensitivity Disclosures, Q4 FY2024 10-K).

**Risk 2 — Basel III Capital Endgame:** The Federal Reserve's revised Basel III endgame rules are expected to impose an approximately 9% increase in risk-weighted capital requirements on the largest U.S. banks — reduced from the original 19% proposal after industry pushback (Source: Federal Reserve Basel III Endgame Proposed Rule, Revised November 2024). Higher capital requirements reduce return on equity structurally and may require dividend growth restraint or buyback pace reductions at Goldman, which operates with a tighter capital buffer than JPMorgan.

**Risk 3 — Consumer Credit Deterioration Acceleration:** The base case is credit normalization rather than crisis-level deterioration, but the downside scenario — unemployment rising above 5%, card delinquencies spiking simultaneously — would require significant incremental provisioning across JPMorgan's and BofA's consumer franchises. This risk is elevated in a scenario where the Fed's rate cuts prove insufficient to prevent economic slowdown after 40 years of the most aggressive tightening cycle.

**Risk 4 — Commercial Real Estate Refinancing Wave:** Approximately $1.5 trillion in U.S. CRE loans mature between 2024 and 2026. Office and retail properties facing the most severe value impairment cannot refinance at original loan-to-value ratios, creating a multi-year charge-off cycle that will keep provision expense elevated even in a benign macro scenario.

**Risk 5 — Geopolitical and Market Dislocation:** Escalating U.S.–China trade tensions, European fiscal fragmentation risk, and tail risks from Middle East conflict spillover represent scenarios in which capital markets activity freezes, credit quality deteriorates, and trading revenues simultaneously disappoint. This correlated downside scenario is the one in which all three banks underperform simultaneously — the scenario that diversified banking sector exposure does not protect against.

## Outlook

The U.S. large-cap banking sector enters the rate-cut phase of the cycle from a position of genuine strength: well-capitalized, modestly provisioned, and generating diversified revenue streams that reduce single-factor vulnerability. According to SEC filings, all three banks maintain Common Equity Tier 1 ratios well above regulatory minimums — JPMorgan at approximately 15.3%, Goldman at approximately 14.6%, and Bank of America at approximately 11.9% as of FY2024 year-end (Source: JPMorgan, Goldman Sachs, Bank of America 10-K FY2024, Capital Ratios Disclosure).

This suggests rate cuts will significantly impact relative performance in FY2025–FY2026 in a way that separates the three banks rather than lifting or depressing them uniformly. JPMorgan's diversification across NII, IB fees, trading, and asset management makes it the most resilient to any single adverse scenario. Goldman is the highest-beta play on IB cycle recovery and should be the primary beneficiary of accelerating M&A and IPO activity in a lower-rate environment. Bank of America offers the most compelling NII recovery option value as its HTM portfolio matures — but that recovery requires patience and carries more duration risk if rates normalize at a level lower than the 4%+ range currently embedded in consensus.

Earnings transcripts confirm that all three management teams have expressed confidence in their capital return programs, with JPMorgan and Goldman actively repurchasing shares at approximately 1.5–2.0x tangible book value — a valuation range that neither reflects distressed pricing nor bubble-level exuberance, and that supports a constructive sector view for investors with a 12–24 month horizon.
